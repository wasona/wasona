You've learned 131 words, and all the important grammar! So what now?

**Join a Toki Pona community!** [ma pona](https://discord.gg/6zkugJFU4x) and [kama sona](https://discord.gg/ChC6qtVsSE) are great places to find people to talk to, and to practice. You can even [hang out in VRChat](https://vrc.group/TOKI.9663)!

**Stay informed!** Every two-four weeks we post a [newsletter](https://sin-kulupu.pona.la/) about new things happening. Sign up so you don't miss cool events, like the [24 hour celebration stream](https://suno.pona.la/) we have every August!

**Listen to spoken Toki Pona!** [kalama sin](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLjOmpMyMxd8Qs2mAXcLk817tQy_AQj09u) is a podcast where you can listen to live conversations between proficient Toki Pona speakers. Turn on subtitles if it's difficult to keep up!

**Read more Toki Pona!** [Practice](https://raacz.neocities.org/toki-pona/beginner-material) by reading ~70 short stories by jan Lakuse. [lipu tenpo](https://liputenpo.org/) publishes a new zine every month or two, with short stories and talks.

And finally, **listen to [Toki Pona music](https://kute.pona.la/)!** We have lots of talented musicians writing original songs or translating songs they love. Let's check out this awesome song *'ale li ante'* by jan Nikoleta (kqa)!

So what is the takeaway from this song? Maybe it's the quirky punctuation that you haven't seen yet in sitelen pona? Or the fact the song comes together nicely even without many rhymes? Or the quite somber but hopeful lyrics? Or maybe you just listen to it a couple times and it's a nice vibe?

The only right answer is the one you find yourself. Not just for the song, of course, but for Toki Pona as a whole. It offers a little something for everyone, and it goes as deep as you want it to be. I hope you enjoy your time with this nice little language.
